“Derivative Narration” is a project that seeks possibility of creative graphic making with machine-generated literature.

The workshop was first made possible with Graphic Design MFA students at Otis College of Art and Design, as part of the Otis GD MFA Art Book Fair, Summer 2023.
Written by Eager Zhang in Hypertext, June 2023:


Why Do We Need To Research On Machine Language Processing At This Moment?


Since my middle school year, I am a longtime lover of linguistics. I was born as a native speaker of Chinese, am using English for working at United States. I used to do sports news translations as a volunteer in Japanese, and knows some grammar knowledge of Russian, Spanish and other languages I randomly picked up. What linguistic studies bring me is, very often in my life, I'm curious about the relationship between language and thinking itself.

Remember when we were all kids. You barely remembered how you started speaking, but you really did, and that definitely made your parents' day from a while ago. You slowly learned how to speak in your mother tongue, most of the time it was gibberish and the adults around your were to train you, with the only method they knew: to tell you right or wrong, and repeat. By mimicking with your ears, you then can talk like a little chatty camedian, and your parents started to teach you how to write your name -- could be a pain in the ass for some Chinese kids to draw that square word monsters. And then, you went to school, when your skills really started to take off: your made huge progress in reading and writing, sometimes because you loved it, but sometimes it's the homework, grading system and grumpy professors that pushed your forward.

Well, this is your journey of learning language, but does it have anything to do with our theme today? Sometimes I look at the ChatGPT, the golden baby of many tech parents behind it, and think: actually, we didn't really exceed the limit of imagination as human being when we created GPTs: we train them just like a human baby. At the beginning we make it mimick us, "us" is a language model. It can be simple just as your mom repeating: "eat-an-apple". And then, we make the program eat more, such as an LLM, which can contain trillions of words, just like you memorized a whole library. We also created a tutor for an AI student, which can tell if it is doing a great job and kick its ass sometimes, so it can move forward itself. A beginner AI writer might only read a few articles and write its own text through Markov process; an "experienced" AI writer might think wisely with its RNN, after "reading" millions of word materials.

If you find my interpretation kinda helpful, but still scary because you're not a tech person and don't know how to start, I'm also in your shoes. When I first saw the AI-generated Harry Potter New Episode, I was caught by the idea of using rules, digits and parameters to write literature, which not only has logic statments, but also contains emotions, styles, narratives, and poetry. But hold on: who said AI is the first being that uses rules to generate subtle emotional interpretations? Talking about poems, from Haiku to Shakespeare Sonnets, human really are into the concept of inventing small machines for the writers to fill in their creativities. Another good example of we treating ourselves as machine could be Raymond Queneau's Exercices de Style: a little passage was rewritten 100 times with different styles just to tell a same story. I see strong connections between these early writing practice with contemporary AI training: poetry is hard and intangible, but we can look for shortcuts to weave it, and even shape readers' mind of preceiving it.

The beauty of machine writing is not simply making it another intelligence just like a human brain. The dynamic, generative nature of a machine will give us unexpected results, which comes from trillions of micro calculations from its silicon neuro cells but when we read it, we use our intuitives, which is technically overthinking (admit it!). Thousands of zombie accounts, created by AI in Chinese social media platform, were wandering around with generated fragmental posts, and collected by the lovers when some of the zombie posts just sound like what Nietzsche would say. Languages are infrastructure of human civilization, but there're moments when language is just itself, just like what the followers of Omnious Signs and Depth of Wikipedia see. My artistic approach of this domain of creating is tied with my identity and thoughts on geographical displacement, you also see other artist like Rayyane Tabet, who utilizes this dual-nature of language and make it into silent monumentals towards the land beneath us.

"We are the only thing in the universe that universe created to understand itself."
(Ricky Gervais, from his Netflix special SuperNature)
