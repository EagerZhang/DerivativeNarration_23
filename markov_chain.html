Intro to Rita Markov Object:
Your First Text Prediction Program
In this tutorial demo, we're trying to understand how a computer program reproduces new text based on one or more text material(s), in a very basic level: not smart like ChatGPT, but at least with correct grammers, great vocabulary choices, and: sounds like the author of your text source -- this is the fun part.
What can I make after I learn this?
- By collecting the text from certain Twitter account, you can make a new bot that, for example, sounds like @god.
- You can combine a few texts together and make some hybrid literature out of it, like a Kafgenstein text.
What do I need:
- Editor: Visual Studio Code, with Live Server extension installed.
- Your web browser: Chrome (most recommended), Safari, or Firefox.
- Language: Javascript.
1.1
What is Markov chain?
Markov chains (also called n-gram models) can be thought of as systems of states and transitions. Informally, this may be thought of as, "What happens next depends only on the state of affairs now."


In HBO TV Series "Good Omens"(2019), one of the main characters, Aziraphale, opened this bookstore at London with an very complex opening hours that are mostly depending on his mood. This transcript here can be seen as a Markov Chain case:

"Bookshop Opening Hours

I open the shop on most weekdays about 9:30 or perhaps 10am. While occasionally I open the shop as early as 8, I have been known not to open until 1, except on Tuesday. I tend to close about 3:30pm, or earlier if something needs tending to. However, I might occasionally keep the shop open until 8 or 9 at night, you never know when you might need some light reading. On days that I am not in, the shop will remain closed. On weekends, I will open the shop during normal hours unless I am elsewhere. Bank holidays will be treated in the usual fashion, with early closing on Wednesdays, or sometimes Fridays. (For Sundays see Tuesdays).

A.Z. Fell, Bookseller"

Create my first text prediction program with RiTa:
using "I Have A Dream".

You can access its full text over here.
Javascript Breaking Down
Full example can be viewed here.

let lines, markov, txt1; let x = 160, y = 240;
- Initial variables.

function preload() { txt1 = loadStrings('ihaveadream.txt'); }
- Load your language materials. Here we use the full text of I have A Dream;

createCanvas(800,600);
textFont('helvetica', 24);
textLeading(28);
textAlign(LEFT);
- Inside your function setup(), here are a few p5 attributes that powers the result from RiTa to be rendered with certain typographic settings.

lines = ["click to (re)generate"];
markov = RiTa.markov(3);
markov.addText(txt1.join(' '));
drawText();
- This is the major call-and-run loop of our program. We use click to generate new text every single time.
- What is the number 3 inside RiTa.markov? This is the n-value of the n-gram. An n value here means how many words as options, that the predicting program will consider as the next node. The larger the n-value gets, the more similar it'll tend to look like the original text.

background(200, 200, 200);
fill(20);
text(lines.join(' '), x, y, 420, 440);
- Is using p5 to define what "drawText" is;

lines = markov.generate(4);
x = y = 40;
drawText();
print(lines);
- The number inside markov.generate() is the number of sentences you'd like to generate with every click;
- x and y here is a margin set up for rendering the paragraph. Unit is px.
- While drawText() basically output an image format from p5 environment, print() allows you to finally grab your text from the console.

What About Some Blending?
This program not only provides you with the possibility of learning from one text resources, but also blends a few different style together.

By easily creating different variables, txt1, txt2, txt3,... you can easily create a multi-style literature.

An example I made blended three text together: I Have A Dream by Martin Luther King Jr., The Waste Land by T.S.Eliot, and The Communist Manifesto by Friedrich Engels and Karl Marx.
